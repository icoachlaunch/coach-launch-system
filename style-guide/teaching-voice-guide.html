▲ The core principle

Teach like the smartest friend who's actually done it. Plain words. Straight to the point. You care AND you push. Never show off — make it useful.

How You Teach

Casual

Talk like a person, not a textbook

"Here's the thing about your offer..." — not "This section addresses offer construction."

Direct

Say the thing. Fast.

"Lock your message first. Everything else leans on it."

Confident

Teach with authority, not hedging

"This is the order. Build it this way." — not "You might want to maybe try..."

Blunt when needed

Call out the mistake plainly

"Skipping this step isn't brave. It's dumb. It'll cost you."

Empathetic but pushy

Care AND push

"I know it feels scary. Do it anyway — here's exactly how."

Specific

Numbers beat vague. Names beat descriptions.

"Close 8–12 clients in 28 days" — not "get great results".

Write at a 5th-Grade Level

If a 12-year-old can't follow it, rewrite it. This isn't dumbing down — it's respect for the reader's time.

  • Short sentences. One idea each.
  • Simple words over fancy ones. "Use", not "utilise".
  • Contractions always — "you'll", "here's", "don't".
  • Talk to one person: "you" and "I".
  • Start sentences with And, But, or So when it flows.
  • Break anything over 3 sentences into shorter chunks.

One or Two Sentences Per Paragraph

This is a hard standard, not a suggestion.

Every paragraph is one sentence — or two, only if they're closely linked.

Then a line break, and white space, before the next one.

Big blocks of text scare the reader off before they start. White space invites them in.

A page that looks easy gets read. A wall of text gets skipped, no matter how good the writing is.

✓ Do this

Your promise names WHO you help.

The Golden Avatar™ goes deep on that person.

You build it from real evidence — not imagination.

✕ Not that

Your promise names who you help, and the Golden Avatar™ goes deep on that person, which you build from real evidence rather than imagination, because a made-up avatar points your marketing at a ghost and wastes ad spend.

  • One idea per paragraph. Two short sentences max.
  • White space between every paragraph — let the page breathe.
  • If a paragraph runs 3+ sentences, split it.
  • Applies everywhere: guides, builder prompts, emails, posts.

Every Part Earns Its Place

When you explain a piece of the system, always hit all three. Miss the third and the reader doesn't feel the stakes.

1 · What

What it is

Plain definition. Kill the common misconception up front.

2 · Why

Why it matters

What it does for them, in the system.

3 · Risk

What breaks downstream

What goes wrong if they get it weak or skip it.

Words You Ban

✕ Corporate jargon — kill on sight

leverage · synergy · optimize · utilise · robust · seamless · holistic · unlock your potential · scale to the next level · best-in-class · at the end of the day

✕ Filler & hedging — cut it

Filler: "It's important to note that...", "In order to", "In today's world...", "Needless to say".
Hedging: maybe, perhaps, sort of, kind of, "I think", "you might possibly".

Do This — Not That

✓ Teaching voice

"Your message names ONE person. Miss that and every ad after it burns money."

✕ Not this

"It's important to note that messaging can be leveraged to optimize audience resonance at scale."

Emojis & Emphasis

Sparing. An emoji or symbol earns its place as a signal — a section marker (▲), a locked gate (✔), a warning (⚠) — never as decoration. Bold the one word that matters, not half the sentence.

Score Your Own Draft

  • Could a 12-year-old follow every sentence?
  • Zero corporate jargon, zero filler, zero hedging?
  • Every claim backed by a number or a name?
  • Every part answers what it is, why it matters, and what breaks?
  • It reads out loud like you're talking to one person?
  • You pushed them — not just informed them?
