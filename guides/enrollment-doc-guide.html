The 7X Red Diamonds — How You Add Huge Value

This is the heart of a high-ticket offer, and the answer to the question every buyer is really asking: why is it worth this much?

The 7X Red Diamonds are seven elements you build into your offer to make it desirable. You don't need all seven — but every one you add, honestly, raises the real and perceived value and makes the price feel small.

◆ Opportunity
Access to something unique they couldn't get on their own — a high-value person to meet, a podcast spot, a rolodex of contacts. You're opening a door, not just teaching a skill.
◆ Proximity
Closeness to you and to a peer group who've already done it. The room they get into is often worth more than the curriculum.
◆ Exclusivity
Selective, not-for-everyone access — a mastermind, a VIP networking group. Scarcity of access is value in itself.
◆ Expert Access
Direct access to you, your team, and any experts you bring to the circle — partners or consultants who add to the discussion or the results.
◆ Turn-Key
Done-for-you and done-with-you systems and templates. Plug-and-play, not "figure it out." You're selling the shortcut, not the homework.
◆ Experience
Delivered as an experience — events, community, retreats, getaways, a VIP dinner at an exclusive restaurant. People pay a premium to feel something, not just learn something.
◆ Accountability
Structure that supports them to actually implement and get things done faster. The result they came for depends on this as much as the information.
⚠ Only stack what's real

The Red Diamonds add value only when they're true. Include an element only if you can really deliver it.

A promised mastermind, retreat, or rolodex that doesn't exist gets exposed on day one — and a stacked-but-fake offer costs you more than a smaller honest one. If you want an element you don't have yet, build toward it; don't write it as if it's live.

▲ Specific beats generic, every time

"Community" is a shrug. "A monthly mastermind call capped at 12 founders, plus a private group where I answer questions daily" is a reason to buy.

The more concrete the detail on each Red Diamond, the more the value shows — and the easier the yes.

The 3 Levels — Your Ascension Ladder

Inside your offer, you show three tiers — each a higher price than the last. You show all three even if most people buy the entry tier, because naming the peak makes the whole offer feel bigger and gives buyers somewhere to climb.

Level 1

Confidence

Your trainings and system, with some basic coaching. The entry tier — enough for them to trust it works and get moving.

Level 2

Acceleration

Results faster — your turn-key and done-with-you delivery layered on top of the training. They move quicker because you've removed the guesswork.

Level 3

Proximity

Direct access to you — the highest-value tier. It's named for Proximity, your top Red Diamond, because closeness to the person who's done it is the most valuable thing you can sell.

▲ Why "Proximity" shows up twice

Proximity is both one of the 7 Red Diamonds and the name of Level 3 — on purpose.

It's a Red Diamond because access to you adds value at any tier. It names the top tier because at the highest level, proximity to you is the offer. Same idea, two roles.

The Builder uses your real prices for each tier — it never invents them. If you only have one offer today, you can still name the ladder and mark Levels 2 and 3 as where you're headed, so the reader sees the vision.

The Value Stack & the Fast Acting Bonus

Once the reader wants the offer, these two blocks make the price feel like a bargain.

The Value Stack

The Value Stack lists every deliverable, each with a real dollar value, totaled — so the gap between the total and the price is impossible to miss.

Your complete Magic Formula™ system is one line item. Each real Red Diamond becomes a line — the mastermind, the retreat, the expert access, the templates. The Fast Acting Bonus is a line. Then the total, then the price, then the bridge: "All of that, today, for [price]."

⚠ Don't inflate — it backfires

If your coaching is worth $6,000, don't slap "$60,000 value" on it. The buyer for a high-ticket offer is smart, and one fake number taints every honest one on the list.

Use real comparisons: what you'd charge for the piece alone, or what the alternative actually costs. A believable gap sells; a fantasy gap kills trust.

✓ Real values

"The complete 90-Day Client System — $6,000. The monthly mastermind (12 seats) — $4,800/yr. Done-with-you templates — $1,200. Total value: $12,000. Yours today: $5,000."

✕ Inflated / vague

"My whole system (worth like $60,000+), plus coaching, plus tons of bonuses — a $97,000 value for just $5,000!"

The Fast Acting Bonus

A real bonus for acting now, with a genuine deadline — join by a date, be one of the first few, decide by the end of the event.

It rewards the decisive and gives the fence-sitter a concrete reason to move today instead of "someday." Like all scarcity in the doc, the deadline has to be real — a fake one is the fastest way to lose a smart buyer.

The Sales-Letter Blocks

Because your Enrollment Doc is a long-form letter, it layers in the direct-response blocks that turn desire into action. These live in the Execution act, around your CTA.

Fork in the Road
A damaging admission (who this is NOT for, when it won't work) plus qualifiers (who's a great fit). It tells the wrong people not to buy — which filters bad-fit clients and makes good-fit clients lean in.
Confidence Filter
The "if you aren't confident this will produce at least [X] return — don't sign up" line. It assumes the system works and puts the only variable on the reader's commitment to themselves.
Objection Crusher
Price justification: the daily-cost breakdown, the comparison to what they've already spent on courses and calls that didn't work, and — where true — "this is my one offer, no upsells hiding behind it."
CTA
The one action — built to the close you locked in your Money Model. Enrollment, Strategy/Audit, or Deposit. Uses your exact price and a link placeholder, never an invented URL.
P.S. / P.P.S.
The P.S. restates the whole offer in condensed form — price, one real proof point, one genuine urgency element, the link. The P.P.S. gives fence-sitters a personal contact. The P.S. is the second-most-read part of any letter.
▲ The damaging admission builds trust, not doubt

It feels risky to tell someone not to buy. It isn't.

The reader has been braced for a hard sell the whole way. When you willingly disqualify people, you break the pattern — and trust jumps right at the moment they're deciding.

Your CTA Adapts to Your Close

The call to action isn't one-size-fits-all. It's built to the close you locked in your Money Model — and the close is set by your phase. There are exactly three; the Builder never invents a fourth.

CloseDefault phaseWhat the CTA does
Enrollment CloseLaunch (P1)They enroll on the spot — a One Day Offer, no call. CTA = the offer + price, an enrollment link, "what happens when you join," and a final-choice line.
Strategy / Audit CloseExecute (P2) · Growth (P3)They book a session and close there — free early, paid as the pitch sharpens. CTA = book the session, stack what they walk away with either way, plant the seed of working together.
Deposit CloseMastery (P4)A $997 deposit buys the high-ticket offer with no call. CTA = place the deposit, terms and next steps crystal clear. The doc carries the close.
▲ For no-call closes, the doc does more of the selling

With the Deposit Close there's no call to carry the objections — so the document carries them.

That's why the offer layer matters most here: for a no-call close, your Red Diamonds, your Value Stack, your guarantee, and your FAQ are the sales conversation.

Write for Two Readers, Then Deploy It Right

Every sales letter has two readers: the deep reader who reads every word, and the scanner who jumps headline to headline for the offer and the price. Serve both.

The top half — headline through the story — runs letter-style, flowing narrative for the deep reader. The bottom half — system, Red Diamonds, offer, CTA — puts a bold header on every section so the scanner can leap straight to the Value Stack and the price.

▲ Length: long enough to answer everything, short enough to finish

Target roughly 2,000–3,000 words — an 8–12 minute read. Under about 1,500 and you haven't handled enough objections. Over about 3,500 and you're teaching a course instead of selling an offer.

How to deploy it (don't get this wrong)

This is where most people leave money on the table, so read it twice.

Present your offer at your event first. The doc amplifies — it does not replace the event.

Your live offer captures the decisive buyers in the room. Your Enrollment Doc captures everyone else — the considerers, the "let me think about it" people, the ones who got distracted, the ones who need to check with a partner.

The flow that works

  • Make your offer at the event. Stack the value, show the price, handle objections, give the next step. Most sales happen here.
  • Drop the doc. "I put my whole offer into one document — the value, the levels, the proof, the guarantee. Link's in the chat."
  • Tell them what to do with it. "Type 'got it' to confirm you have it. Read it carefully. If you're 100% sure this is for you, I'll see you inside."
  • Let it work after. Everyone who didn't buy in the room now has everything they need to decide on their own time.
⚠ Hiding the price and sending people to "go read the doc" tanks live sales

Defer pricing to a document instead of presenting it live, and the event loses its climax — the people who'd have bought on the spot never get the chance.

Present live. Then amplify with the doc. The one exception is a true no-call Deposit Close, where the doc carries more of the weight — but it still runs alongside a real event, never instead of one.

Common Mistakes & Fixes

Almost every weak Enrollment Doc fails in one of the ways below.

1. A thin offer with no Red Diamonds

What it looks like: The offer is "my program + some calls" — just information, no access, no experience, no exclusivity.

Why it happens: Building real value takes more thought than listing modules.

Why it costs you: Information is cheap and the buyer knows it. Without the Red Diamonds, there's nothing to justify a high-ticket price.

The fix: Stack the Red Diamonds you can really deliver — proximity, expert access, experience, accountability. That's what makes $5K–$10K feel like a deal.

2. Presenting the offer before the arc

What it looks like: The doc opens with the price and the deliverables.

Why it happens: You're proud of the offer, so you lead with it.

Why it costs you: Without the problems, the story, and the system first, the offer reads as a pitch — and pitches get skimmed and closed.

The fix: Keep the SCORE™ order. Earn the yes through Serendipity, Connection, and Opportunity before the reader ever sees the price.

3. Faking value to look bigger

What it looks like: A promised retreat, mastermind, or "$97,000 value" that isn't real.

Why it happens: A bigger-looking offer feels more sellable.

Why it costs you: It gets exposed on day one, and it poisons trust in everything else you claimed. A smaller honest offer outsells a big fake one.

The fix: Only stack what you can deliver. If you want an element you don't have yet, build toward it — don't write it as if it's live.

4. Burying the price

What it looks like: The reader has to hunt for the number, or it's hidden behind "book a call to find out."

Why it happens: Stating the price feels scary, so you soften it.

Why it costs you: A hidden price reads as a trap. Once you've built the value, showing the price plainly is what feels honest — and honesty converts.

The fix: Build the Value Stack, then state the price clearly right after. Let the Objection Crusher and FAQ handle the rest.

5. Trying to close everyone

What it looks like: No Fork in the Road, no qualifiers — the doc chases anyone with a pulse.

Why it happens: Disqualifying people feels like leaving money on the table.

Why it costs you: Chasing everyone attracts bad-fit buyers who churn and complain, and it reads as desperate to the good-fit buyers you actually want.

The fix: Keep the damaging admission and the qualifiers. Strong filters attract better clients and lower refunds.

6. Using the doc to replace the live offer

What it looks like: "I won't cover pricing today — it's all in the doc, go read it."

Why it happens: Presenting price live feels uncomfortable, so the doc becomes a hiding place.

Why it costs you: The event loses its climax and the on-the-spot buyers never buy. The doc is an amplifier, not a replacement.

The fix: Present the offer live, then drop the doc to catch the rest. For a no-call Deposit Close, the doc does more — but still runs alongside a real event.

The Enrollment Doc isn't scored out of 25 — and that's on purpose.

The SCORE™ Card it's built from was already gated with a Ready Check, and the Money Model already cleared its Day-7 profitability gate. So the right test here is a completion gate plus a pass/fail Ready Check — the same family as the SCORE™ Card and the $Million Moment™.

The completion gate — before anything assembles

The Builder refuses to assemble the letter until every required block exists and is approved: the 7X Red Diamonds, the 3 Levels, the Fast Acting Bonus, the Value Stack, the Fork in the Road, the Confidence Filter, the CTA, the Objection Crusher, the FAQ, and the P.S./P.P.S. (Risk Reversal and FOMO are included only if they're genuine.)

This exists because a letter with the story but no offer layer looks finished and converts poorly. The gate stops a partial doc from shipping.

The Ready Check — run every section through this

  • The SCORE™ flow: the letter runs S → C → O → R → E in order; the narrative blocks match your locked card word-for-word; the full system (all 9 steps) is present.
  • 7X Red Diamonds: only the real ones, each with a specific, deliverable detail — none faked.
  • 3 Levels: three tiers, contents + your real prices, lowest to highest; the core Money Model price matches its tier.
  • Value Stack & bonus: real deliverables and values, complete system as one line, Red Diamonds as line items, total makes the price feel small; the bonus has a genuine deadline.
  • Fork & Confidence Filter: a true damaging admission with specific qualifiers; the filter names your real currency, delivery model, and price.
  • CTA: matches your locked close exactly, correct price, link placeholder — never an invented URL.
  • Objection Crusher & FAQ: honest comparisons, correct daily-cost math, real objections answered from your Golden Avatar™.
  • Risk Reversal / FOMO: real guarantee and genuine scarcity — or honestly absent, never manufactured.
  • Whole doc: represents the complete offer; ~2,000–3,000 words; hybrid formatting; no legacy terms; ™ on every coined name; Matthew's figures never inside your doc.

Gate passed and every section checks out? Your Enrollment Doc is ready to deploy.
