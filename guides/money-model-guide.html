What Is the Money Model?

Your Money Model is the math that turns your system into a business.

It's the decision layer under your offer: which path you're on, what you charge, how the cash comes in, and how people actually enroll.

Put simply — it's the answer to the one question that decides everything else: how does this actually make money?

It matters because a great message and a great system can still go broke.

You can have the sharpest $Million Promise™ and the best Magic Formula™ in your market, and still lose money every month — if the model underneath them is wrong.

Price it too low and you bleed. Sell it the wrong way and you burn out on a launch treadmill. Skip the cash-flow math and your ad money runs out before a client ever pays you.

Get the model right and the opposite happens. The price fits the promise, the events pay for themselves, and every dollar you put in comes back with friends.

This is the first half of your Red Diamond Offer™ — the strategy before the packaging.

We build the model here. We write it up as an offer in Part 2.

What the Money Model is NOT

It helps to be clear about what this isn't, because most people skip it or fake it.

It's not a guess or a gut feeling about price — "I think I can charge $5K" is a wish, not a model.

It's not your offer document — that's Part 2. This is the strategy the document will describe.

It's not a fixed thing you set once forever — your model grows as you do, phase by phase.

And it's not the same for everyone — the right model depends entirely on where you are today.

Here's the gut-check: if you can't show the math for how one dollar of effort or ad spend comes back as more than a dollar, you don't have a model yet — you have a hope.

Why Most Money Models Fail

Before what works, here's what doesn't — the three ways coaches price and sell themselves into a hole.

1

Too cheap to survive

The $27 workshop, the $97 mini-course, the cheap-and-easy offer.

It feels safe because the price is low and the "yes" is easy. But the math is brutal — if it costs you real money to get a lead and only a few percent buy something cheap, you lose money on every dollar you spend to promote it.

And cheap buyers are the least committed. They don't show up, don't finish, and don't become the results that grow your business. Low price isn't a soft start — it's a slow bleed.

2

Stuck in the dead zone

The $500–$3,000 course or program.

Too expensive to buy on impulse, too cheap to justify a real sales conversation. So you "launch" — a big push, a spike of sales — and then month two resets to zero and you do it all again.

That's not a business. It's a treadmill you have to sprint on forever, and it's why so many talented coaches burn out.

3

High ticket, broken machine

The $5K–$10K offer sold the old way: free calls, no-shows, tire-kickers, and a "float period" where your ad money goes out for weeks before a dollar comes back.

The price is right. The machine around it is exhausting and cash-starved.

Most people quit here not because the offer was bad, but because they ran out of money and nerve before payday arrived.

▲ The real lesson

Notice the pattern: it's never just the price.

It's the price, plus how the cash comes in, plus how people enroll. That trio is your money model — and the Coach Launch model fixes all three at once.

The Coach Launch Money Model

The model that fixes all three failures rests on one line: the deeper the immersion, the higher the conversion.

Instead of a cheap product, a launch treadmill, or a free-call grind, you run an event.

People come, you deliver a real win from your Magic Formula™, and the offer at the end feels like the obvious next step — because they've just experienced you, not just read about you.

Three moving parts make the money work.

1. The event does the selling

One-to-many. You teach a genuine win, and the offer lands warm instead of cold. Deeper events — longer, multi-day — earn higher prices and higher close rates.

2. The tickets and VIP pay for the traffic

This is the Cash Flow Engine™ principle: you charge a small amount for a seat or a VIP upgrade, and that cash covers the ad spend that filled the room.

So your leads are basically free — and they're buyers, not browsers. (You build this in full in Step 5 · Cash Flow Engine™.)

3. The offer is priced to the phase

You don't guess the price. You match it to where you are on the map — because the event you can run today determines the price you can honestly charge today.

That's the key that most people miss. Your money model isn't one fixed thing forever. It's the right model for your current phase — and it grows as you do.

Pricing Your Offer

Price isn't a feeling. It's math wrapped around a promise.

Inside your phase band, two checks decide where you land.

Does the price match the promise? A bigger outcome carries a bigger price. If your $Million Promise™ is life-changing, don't price it like a weekend workshop.

Can you defend it out loud? If you can't say the number without flinching, that's usually a proof problem, not a price problem. Start lower in the band and raise it as your proof and testimonials grow — that's exactly what the phases are for.

One hard rule the Builder holds you to: it never changes your number. You pick the price; the math tells you the truth about it.

The Math (and the Gate)

Every projection shows the formula, not just the answer — so you can see exactly where every number comes from.

And every projection runs in three scenarios: Low (a cold start), Realistic (the most likely case), and High (what's possible as you sharpen). The Realistic column is the one that counts.

The core formulas

Leads = Ad spend ÷ CPL
Attendees = Registrants × Show rate
Cash-in = Buyers × (Ticket + VIP + bumps)
Net traffic cost = Ad spend − Cash-in
Offer revenue = Attendees × Conversion × Price
Day-7 return = Day-7 cash ÷ Ad spend  ← the gate

The profitability gate

Here's the line that keeps you honest: at the Realistic scenario, your Day-7 return has to be at least 1.0x.

In plain words — within the first seven days, the cash coming in (deposits, first payments, and ticket/VIP cash-in) has to at least equal what you spent to get those clients.

If it doesn't, the Builder stops you. Not to be difficult — to save you from a model that quietly loses money on every cycle.

When the gate fails you've got three honest fixes: raise the price inside your band, deepen the event to earn a higher price and close, or lower the ad spend and lean on organic to fill the room. You choose; then you rerun the math.

▲ Why Day 7, not Day 90

Plenty of models look great over 90 days and still kill the business.

Because the bills — the ad spend — come due in week one, long before the "eventually" revenue shows up. If the model pays fast, you can keep running it. If it only pays slowly, you run out of cash first. Day 7 is the honest test.

Offer Mechanics: Cash-In & the Close

The second half of Part 1 is the mechanics — how cash comes in, and how the client actually says yes.

Cash-in

The ticket, the VIP upgrade, and any order bumps for your phase. This is what self-liquidates your ad spend, so your leads are buyers, not browsers.

The close — three directions

There isn't one way to close. There are three, and you pick the one that fits your offer and your phase.

The Strategy / Audit Close is the one that deepens: it starts free while you're proving the offer, then goes paid as your pitch sharpens — because a paid session filters for serious buyers who show and close far better.

How the close maps to your phase

You choose the close, but each phase has a natural default — and the close deepens as you climb.

  • Phase 1 · Launch → Enrollment Close into your One Day Offer.
  • Phase 2 · Execute → Strategy / Audit Close (free) into your Beta Offer ($3K+).
  • Phase 3 · Growth → Strategy / Audit Close (paid) into your Full Offer ($5K+).
  • Phase 4 · Mastery → Deposit Close into your Full Offer ($10K+) — high ticket, no call.

That's the default ladder. When you graduate a phase, your close changes with it — so you rerun your model and lock the new one.

The whole sequence, start to finish:

Traffic  →  Event (ticket / VIP cash-in)  →  the win from your Magic Formula™  →  the offer  →  your close  →  paid client

Notice the pattern: the more someone pays to get in front of you — a ticket, a VIP seat, a paid session, a deposit — the more serious they are, the higher they show, and the more they buy.

That's the immersion rule again, applied to the close. Free is fine when you're starting; paid filters for buyers as you climb.

Common Mistakes & Fixes

Almost every broken money model fails in one of the ways below.

1. Pricing on feelings, not the promise

What it looks like: "I'll charge $497 because that feels safe" — with no link to the outcome.

Why it happens: A low number feels less scary to say out loud.

Why it costs you: A price that's too small for the promise reads as "this probably won't work," and the math rarely clears the gate.

The fix: Price to the promise and the phase band. If you can't defend the number yet, start lower in the band and raise it as your proof grows.

2. Selling above your phase

What it looks like: Pitching a $10K offer off a 30-minute freebie with no track record.

Why it happens: The big price is tempting, and skipping steps feels faster.

Why it costs you: There's not enough immersion or proof to earn that price, so it doesn't close — and you blame the offer.

The fix: Match the offer to the event to the phase. Earn your way up as your proof and your event depth grow.

3. Ignoring the cash-in

What it looks like: Free everything — no ticket, no VIP — so 100% of the ad spend is a naked cost.

Why it happens: "Free fills more seats" feels obviously right.

Why it costs you: Without cash-in, nothing self-liquidates your traffic, and the Day-7 gate gets much harder to clear.

The fix: Add the phase-appropriate ticket or VIP. Even a small cash-in shrinks your real cost of traffic and filters for buyers.

4. Judging on the best case

What it looks like: "At a 10% conversion this makes a fortune!"

Why it happens: The high scenario is the fun one to look at.

Why it costs you: You build a business on your best week and go broke on your average one.

The fix: Judge the model on the realistic middle scenario. If it pays there, the good weeks are a bonus.

5. Forgetting the float

What it looks like: A model that "works over 90 days" but bleeds cash for the first six weeks.

Why it happens: You add up the eventual revenue and ignore when the bills actually land.

Why it costs you: The ad spend is due in week one; the slow revenue can't save you if you're already out of cash.

The fix: Clear the Day-7 gate. A model that pays fast survives; one that only pays slowly runs out of runway.

6. Treating benchmarks as guarantees

What it looks like: Building a plan on "$15 leads and 40% show" as if they're promised.

Why it happens: Round default numbers feel solid.

Why it costs you: Your real numbers will differ, and a plan built on borrowed assumptions cracks the moment reality shows up.

The fix: Use the ranges to start, then replace every one with your own measured numbers as fast as you can.
