What Is the Business Brand Profile?

Your Business Brand Profile is the foundation your whole business stands on — written down in one place.

It's a short, clear document that answers the questions everything else depends on.

Who are you? What do you stand for? Who do you serve? What do you sell, and where are you headed?

It comes in two halves.

The first half is who you are — what you do, your mission, your values, your positioning, your personality, and your brand promise.

The second half is your business snapshot — your offer, your model, your market, your stage, and your goals.

It matters because it's the ground every other decision is built on.

Your look comes from your personality. Your voice comes from your values and personality. Your $Million Promise™ comes from your market and positioning.

When the foundation is clear, all of that gets easier and more consistent.

When it's fuzzy, you feel it everywhere — in ads that don't sound like you, posts that wander, and offers that don't fit the person you actually serve.

This isn't a branding exercise about logos and colors — that comes next, in your Visual Style Guide.

This is about getting honest and specific about the business underneath all of it.

What the Business Brand Profile is NOT

It helps to be clear about what this isn't, because it's easy to confuse a few things.

It's not your $Million Promise™. That's the sharp marketing hook you build in Step 1 of the Formula. Your Profile holds your brand promise — the experience standard every client can count on. Different jobs: one is what you say first, one is who you are.

It's not your Visual Style Guide or your Brand Voice — those are the two foundations you build next, and they both grow out of this one.

It's not a mission statement you frame and forget. It's a working document you actually use to make decisions.

And it's not a fantasy version of your business. It's the true one, said plainly — because everything downstream inherits whatever you write here.

Part 1 — Who You Are, In Full

1

What You Do — the one line

Your one line is the plain-language version of what you do for the people you serve.

The way you'd say it to a stranger at a barbecue: "I help ___ do ___."

Why it matters. Everything hangs off this. If you can't say it plainly, you can't sell it, market it, or build on it.

How to find yours

Finish "I help [who] [do what]" with a specific person and a specific outcome. No jargon, no mission-speak — the thing your best client would tell their friend you did for them.

✓ Do this

"I help new moms get their energy back without giving up coffee or sleep." — a real person, a real outcome.

✕ Not that

"I optimize human potential through holistic transformation." — nobody knows what you actually do.

The trap: dressing up the one line to sound impressive. Plain wins. If a 12-year-old wouldn't get it, cut it back.

2

Mission — why this exists

Your mission is the reason the business exists beyond making money — the change you're trying to make.

Why it matters. Mission is what keeps you consistent when things get hard, and what makes the right people trust you. It's the "why" people can feel underneath the "what."

How to find yours

Name the specific change you care about, for your specific people. What problem gets under your skin? What would still matter to you even if the money was handled?

✓ Do this

"To make sure no first-time mom feels like she has to choose between her health and her family."

✕ Not that

"To empower people to become the best version of themselves." — could be any business alive.

The trap: reaching for a poster-worthy line. A mission that could belong to anyone belongs to no one. Get specific about your change and your people.

3

Values — what you stand for

Your values are the 3–5 beliefs you actually run your business by — the ones that decide how you act when it's hard.

Why it matters. Values are how customers feel the difference between you and the next option. They also make your own decisions easier, because you've decided in advance what you won't do.

How to find yours

Pick 3 to 5, and for each, write the one-line version of what it actually means to you — because "integrity" means something different to everyone. Keep only the ones you'd never break.

✓ Do this

"Honesty over hype — I'll tell you if my program isn't right for you, even if it costs me the sale."

✕ Not that

"Integrity. Excellence. Passion." — wallpaper words with no meaning attached.

The trap: listing nice words with nothing behind them. A value with no definition is decoration. Give each one a sentence that shows up in how you work.

4

Positioning — the lane you own

Positioning is two things: the lane you're in, and what makes you different in it.

Why it matters. If you look like everyone else on the shelf, price becomes the only thing left to compete on. A real difference is what lets you charge more and be remembered.

How to find yours

First, name your category — the shelf a customer would find you on. Then name the one thing you do, believe, or deliver that most competitors don't. If you're stuck, ask your best clients what they can't get anywhere else.

✓ Do this

"Fitness coaching for moms — the only program built around 15-minute workouts you do with your kids, not away from them."

✕ Not that

"Fitness coaching — but I really care about my clients." — everyone says that. It's not a difference.

The trap: claiming "I care more" or "I'm more experienced" as your difference. Those aren't differences — they're what everyone claims. Find the thing that's true for you and not for them.

5

Personality — how your brand behaves

Your brand personality is how your brand acts if it walked into a room — the direction plus a few true traits.

Why it matters. This is the north star for your look and your voice. Your colors, fonts, and tone all come from here, so a clear personality keeps them consistent.

How to find yours

Pick a direction that fits — the Mentor, the Challenger, the Friend, the Expert, the Motivator, the Rebel, the Creator — or blend two. Then add 3 adjectives that are actually true of how you show up. Bold, warm, precise, playful, calm — pick the real ones, not the impressive ones.

✓ Do this

"The Challenger — bold, direct, a little funny." — clear, and you can build a look and voice on it.

✕ Not that

"Professional, approachable, innovative, trustworthy, dynamic, and fun." — six adjectives that cancel each other out.

The trap: picking the personality you wish you had. Build on how you actually show up — it's more magnetic than a costume, and you can keep it up.

6

Brand Promise — what they can always count on

Your brand promise is the experience standard every client gets, every time — the thing you'd never let slide.

Why it matters. Results vary from client to client, but your standard shouldn't. The brand promise is what builds trust and referrals, because people know exactly what they'll get from working with you.

▲ Brand promise ≠ $Million Promise™

These get confused, so keep them straight.

Your brand promise (here) is your standard — how you show up. "You'll always get an honest answer within 24 hours."

Your $Million Promise™ (Step 1 of the Formula) is your marketing hook — the outcome that stops a stranger scrolling. Two different jobs.

How to find yours

Finish: "Every client who works with me can always count on ___." Focus on how you deliver — responsiveness, honesty, depth, care — not on a result you can't guarantee.

✓ Do this

"You'll never be left guessing — every question gets a straight answer within a day."

✕ Not that

"You'll make $10,000 a month." — that's an outcome, and outcomes vary. It's not a standard you control.

The trap: promising a result instead of a standard. Promise the thing you can keep on your worst week — that's what makes it real.

Part 2 — Your Business Snapshot, In Full

7

Offer — what you sell

Your offer is what's actually on the menu today — your main offer (or offers) and roughly what each costs.

Why it matters. Your foundation should reflect the real business, not a someday version. Naming the main offer keeps the whole profile anchored to how you actually earn.

How to find yours

Name the main offer — the one that makes most of your money, or the one you're building toward. Don't worry about pricing strategy; that's the Money Model later. If you're pre-offer, write the one you're building and mark it "in progress."

The trap: listing your whole catalog. Anchor on the one core offer — clarity beats completeness here.

8

Model — how you make money

Your model is how the money comes in: coaching, group program, course, membership, done-for-you, events, or a mix — and whether it's low, mid, or high-ticket.

Why it matters. Your model shapes everything from your content to your Money Model later. Naming it plainly keeps expectations honest.

How to find yours

Pick the core engine today. If you run several models, name the one that carries the business right now — that's your anchor.

The trap: listing five models because you "do a bit of everything." Name the one that pays the bills today.

9

Market — who you serve

Your market is the headline version of who you sell to — the type of person or business, and whether it's B2C or B2B.

Why it matters. This is the seed your Golden Avatar™ grows from. Get the headline right and the deep-dive later is far easier.

How to find yours

Say it in a sentence — the core person or business you serve. You don't need the full portrait yet; you build that in your Golden Avatar™. Just name the bullseye.

The trap: answering "everyone." Even at the headline level, pick the core one — you can widen honestly later.

10

Where You Are — your stage

This marks the phase you're standing in today, using the four phases of The $100K Day Accelerator™.

PhaseYou're validatingHeading for
LaunchYour messageFirst 3–5 paying clients
ExecuteYour offerFirst $25,000/month
GrowthYour pitchFirst $50,000/month
MasteryScaleFirst $100,000/month

Why it matters. Knowing your phase sizes the rest of your build correctly. You earn your way up — starting where you actually are is what makes the climb work.

The trap: picking Mastery on day one because it sounds better. Be honest — the phase you're really in is the one that gives you the right next move.

11

Goals — where you're headed

Two goals: your 12-month target (with a number) and your bigger 3-to-5-year vision.

Why it matters. A number you can aim at turns a wish into a plan. The bigger vision keeps the near-term grind connected to something that matters.

How to find yours

Put a real number or milestone on the 12-month goal — revenue, clients, whatever matters most. Then name the bigger picture: freedom, impact, a team, a certain life. Money's fine, but let the human version in too.

The trap: "grow" as a goal. Grow to what? Give the year a number you can hit or miss.

A Worked Example

Here's the difference between a foundation that says nothing and one that could only belong to one business.

The vague version

"I'm a health coach who helps people live healthier, happier lives through personalized wellness plans. My mission is to empower everyone to reach their full potential. I value integrity, excellence, and passion."

It sounds fine, and it says nothing. You could paste it onto ten thousand other coaches.

Now the same coach, sharpened.

What you do: "I help new moms get their energy back without giving up sleep or coffee."

Mission: "No first-time mom should have to choose between her health and her family."

Values: Honesty over hype · Small wins over overhauls · Real food over rules.

Positioning: Health coaching for new moms — built around 15-minute routines you do with your baby, not away from them.

Personality: The Friend — warm, real, a little funny.

Brand promise: "You'll never feel judged, and every plan fits a real mom's day."

Same coach. But now a designer knows what it looks like, a copywriter knows how it sounds, and a new mom knows it's for her.

The Hard Rules

These keep your foundation true and specific enough to build on, and the Builder holds you to them.

Plain over polished. Every element should be said the way a real person would say it. If it needs decoding, it's not done.

Specific over safe. If an answer could belong to a thousand other businesses, it's not yours yet. Push for the version only you could write.

True over aspirational. Build on how your business actually is, not how you wish it looked. The real version is more magnetic and easier to keep.

Values with meaning. 3–5 values, each with a one-line definition. No bare word lists.

A real difference. Positioning needs a difference that's true for you and not for your competitors — never "I care more."

Brand promise, not outcome. Promise the standard you control, not a result that varies.

An honest phase. Mark where you actually are in the Accelerator, not where you wish you were.

A number on the year. Your 12-month goal needs a figure or milestone you can aim at.

Common Mistakes & Fixes

Almost every weak profile falls into one of these.

1. The everything-to-everyone foundation

What it looks like: "I help people live better lives" with a market of "anyone who wants to grow."

Why it happens: Narrowing feels like turning away business, so you keep it wide.

Why it costs you: A brand for everyone connects with no one, and every layer above it inherits the fog.

The fix: pick the specific person and the specific change. Narrow is what makes people feel seen.

2. Wallpaper values

What it looks like: "Integrity. Excellence. Passion." — a bare list.

Why it happens: Those words feel safe and expected, so they're the first ones you reach for.

Why it costs you: Values with no meaning attached don't guide any decision or signal any difference.

The fix: give each value a one-line definition that shows up in how you actually work.

3. "I care more" as positioning

What it looks like: "What makes me different is I really care about my clients."

Why it happens: It's true and it feels meaningful, so it seems like enough.

Why it costs you: Every competitor says the exact same thing, so it doesn't set you apart at all.

The fix: find the thing that's true for you and NOT for most competitors — a method, a focus, a person, a result.

4. Confusing brand promise with a result

What it looks like: a brand promise of "you'll make six figures."

Why it happens: The big outcome feels like the strongest thing to promise.

Why it costs you: Results vary, so a result-as-promise is one you'll eventually break — and a broken promise costs trust.

The fix: promise the standard you always control — how you show up — and save the outcome for your $Million Promise™.

5. The aspirational personality

What it looks like: picking "bold and provocative" when you're actually warm and steady.

Why it happens: A louder personality seems like it'll get more attention.

Why it costs you: You can't keep up a costume, so your look and voice end up inconsistent and off.

The fix: build on how you genuinely show up. The real you is easier to sustain and more magnetic.

6. Skipping the number on your goal

What it looks like: a 12-month goal of "grow the business."

Why it happens: A specific number feels like pressure you might not meet.

Why it costs you: A goal with no number can't be aimed at, tracked, or hit — so it quietly gets ignored.

The fix: put a real figure or milestone on the year, even a rough one. Aim at something.

Is It Ready?

This foundation isn't scored out of 25 — it's the truth about your business, written plainly. So it passes a Ready Check instead: every item a clear yes.

  • Your one line names a specific who and a specific what, in plain words.
  • Your mission is a real, specific change — not a generic poster.
  • You have 3–5 values, each with a meaning that isn't wallpaper.
  • Your positioning has a clear lane AND a real difference — not "I care more."
  • Your personality is one direction plus 3 true adjectives.
  • Your brand promise is a standard you can always keep — separate from your $Million Promise™.
  • You've named your main offer, your model, and your headline market (B2C or B2B).
  • You've marked your honest Accelerator phase.
  • Your 12-month goal has a number, and you've named the bigger vision.
