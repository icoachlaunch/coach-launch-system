What Is the Brand Voice?

Your Brand Voice is how your brand sounds in words — written down so everything you publish sounds like the same person.

It's your tone, your rhythm, the words you reach for, and the words you'd never use.

The difference between copy that sounds like you and copy that sounds like a committee.

It matters because people buy from brands that sound like a real person.

Your voice is how a stranger decides if they like you before they ever talk to you.

A clear, consistent voice gets your emails opened, your posts read, and your sales pages felt.

A blurry voice — "professional yet approachable," like everyone else — sounds like no one, and no one leans in to no one.

Here's what makes a voice usable instead of just nice-sounding.

A voice you can't copy is a voice you can't keep. Three adjectives on a page won't help you write a single email.

So a real Brand Voice gets concrete: actual word lists, actual rules, actual before-and-after examples, and a sample paragraph a writer — or an AI — can copy exactly.

1
One voice, everywhere. Once it's documented, you can paste it into any AI writing tool and get copy that sounds like you — not like a robot borrowed your logo.

And like your look, your voice isn't random — it comes from who you are.

That's why this is Foundation 3, built from the personality and values in your Business Brand Profile. Your voice is those things, turned into words.

What the Brand Voice is NOT

It's not a list of three adjectives. That's the start, not the guide — the guide is everything concrete underneath them.

It's not one specific piece of copy. It's the rules every piece of copy follows.

It's not Matthew's voice, or any guru's voice, borrowed onto your brand. It's yours — pulled from how you actually talk.

And it's not your Visual Style Guide. That's how you look; this is how you sound. Together they make one recognizable brand.

How You'll Build It

You build a voice in seven layers, going from the general feel down to copy-paste-able specifics.

The Builder walks you through each one — and it works best if you paste in something you've actually written, so it can hear your real voice instead of inventing one.

The layers: your voice attributes (how you sound), your tone range (how it flexes), your signature style (your habits), your words (use and avoid), your grammar and formatting rules, your do-this-not-that examples, and your gold-standard sample.

The rest of this guide teaches each layer in full — what it is, why it matters, how to find yours, and the trap that keeps voices generic.

How you sound + the words you use + a sample to copy = a voice anyone (or any AI) can keep.

The 7 Layers, In Full

1

Voice Attributes — how you sound

Your voice attributes are the 3–5 words that describe how your brand sounds — each with a line on what it means and what it doesn't.

Why it matters. These are the anchor for everything else. But only if they're specific — "professional, approachable, trustworthy" describes a printer, not a person.

How to find yours

Push past the safe words to the true ones: blunt, warm, fiery, nerdy, calm, funny. Pick the flavor only you have, and make sure at least one has an edge — a voice that's all warm has no spine. Then pin each one down: "Direct means I say the hard thing kindly; it does NOT mean I'm harsh." That second line keeps the voice from sliding into caricature.

✓ Do this

"Blunt, warm, a little funny." — you can hear it. And "blunt means honest, not mean" keeps it in check.

✕ Not that

"Professional, approachable, trustworthy." — table stakes. Every brand claims them; none of them are a voice.

The trap: reaching for the respectable words because the true ones feel risky. The risky ones are exactly what make people feel something.

2

Tone Range — how it flexes

Your voice stays the same; your tone shifts with the moment. Tone range names how you sound when you sell, teach, support, and celebrate.

Why it matters. The same tone for every situation reads as flat or, worse, wrong — a comforting message shouldn't sound like a hard sell. Naming the shifts keeps you human across contexts.

How to find yours

For each context, name a couple of words. Selling might be confident and urgent; teaching, patient and plain; supporting, warm and reassuring; celebrating, high-energy. Make sure each still fits your core voice — a blunt brand sells hard without turning into a con.

✓ Do this

Selling: confident, direct. Supporting: gentle, steady. Same voice, different energy for the room.

✕ Not that

The exact same tone whether you're closing a sale or comforting someone who's stuck. It'll land wrong somewhere.

The trap: a selling tone that breaks character — suddenly aggressive on an otherwise warm brand. Flex the energy, not the identity.

3

Signature Style — your fingerprints

Your signature style is the set of habits that make a line unmistakably yours — sentence length, contractions, sentence starters, one-line paragraphs, humor, and any phrase you always use.

Why it matters. These are what people recognize even when they don't know why. They're the difference between "sounds like a brand" and "sounds like you."

How to find yours

Decide your rhythm: short and punchy, flowing and warm, or a mix. Use contractions or not. Start sentences with "And" or "But," or don't. Talk to the reader with questions, or don't. And hunt for your signature phrase — the thing your clients would say is "so you." Keep it; that's gold, not something to polish out.

✓ Do this

"Short sentences. Contractions. One-line paragraphs. And I always say 'here's the thing.'"

✕ Not that

No habits named — so every writer (and every AI) guesses, and the copy never sounds consistent.

The trap: style choices that fight your attributes — "punchy" on paper but long, winding sentences in practice. Make the habits match the voice.

4

Words You Use / Avoid — the vocabulary

Two real lists: the power words you reach for, and the banned words that are never you.

Why it matters. Word choice carries voice faster than almost anything. Swapping a few cringe words for your real ones instantly makes copy sound more like you.

How to find yours

Write 8–15 power words — the verbs, feelings, and audience language you actually use. Then write your banned list: the jargon and clichés that make you cringe ("synergy," "unlock," "game-changer," "crush it" — whatever isn't you). Actual words, not categories like "positive language."

✓ Do this

Use: "build, land, real, stuck, worth it, here's the thing." Avoid: "leverage, unlock, synergy, elevate."

✕ Not that

"Use positive, uplifting words and avoid negative ones." — no actual words, so nobody can apply it.

The trap: listing categories instead of words. "Encouraging language" isn't usable. Give the actual words you'd type.

5

Grammar & Formatting — the mechanics

The mechanical rules: reading level, paragraph length, punctuation, capitalization, emoji policy, and how you handle numbers.

Why it matters. Mechanics are what keep every piece consistent — and they're essential when an AI writes for you, because it needs the rules spelled out. Boring, but they do a lot of quiet work.

How to find yours

Set your reading level (plain and simple usually wins online). Decide paragraph length — short with white space reads easiest. Make punctuation calls: em-dashes, exclamation points, all-caps for emphasis. Pick Title Case or sentence case for headings. Set an emoji policy. And make sure it all lines up with your voice — "plain and punchy" shouldn't come with elevated vocabulary and dense paragraphs.

✓ Do this

"5th-grade level. Short paragraphs. Em-dashes yes, exclamation points rarely. No emojis."

✕ Not that

Rules that contradict the voice — "casual and punchy" but formal grammar and long blocks.

The trap: skipping this because it's dull. It's the layer that makes AI-written copy actually match you — don't leave it blank.

6

Do This / Not That — the proof

Three or four before-and-after pairs: the same idea written off-voice, then in your voice.

Why it matters. Examples make a voice something you can see, not just describe. A single good before/after teaches more than a page of adjectives.

How to find yours

Take normal marketing lines — an offer, a tip, a welcome — and rewrite each using your attributes, power words, and rules. Keep only the pairs you genuinely recognize as yours. If the "after" doesn't sound like you, it's not done.

✕ Off-voice: "We are excited to offer a comprehensive solution to help you optimize your results."

✓ On-voice: "Here's the thing — you don't need more tactics. You need one that actually works. That's what this is."

Same offer. One sounds like a brochure; one sounds like a person you'd listen to.

The trap: locking a polished "after" that isn't actually how you'd say it. Real beats impressive.

7

Gold-Standard Sample — the reference

One short paragraph written fully in your voice — the master reference a writer or an AI copies from.

Why it matters. This is the single most useful thing in the guide. Paste it into any tool and say "write like this," and you get your voice back.

How to find yours

Pick something you'd write often — a welcome, an offer intro, a pep talk — and draft it using every layer above. Then tweak it until you'd happily send it. When you read it and think "yeah, that's me," it's done.

The trap: settling for "close enough." This is the one people copy forever — get it all the way to unmistakably you.

A Worked Example

Here's a no-nonsense business coach turning "how she talks" into a voice she can repeat.

Attributes: Blunt (honest, not mean) · Warm (I'm on your side) · Funny (dry, never goofy).

Signature style: short sentences, contractions, one-line paragraphs, starts with "Look" or "Here's the thing."

Power words: build, real, stuck, worth it, do the work. Banned: leverage, unlock, synergy, hustle.

Sample: "Look — you're not stuck because you're lazy. You're stuck because nobody showed you the order to do things in. Let's fix that. It's simpler than you think, and it's worth it."

Now every email she writes — or any AI writes for her — sounds exactly like that. Consistent, and unmistakably her.

(Illustrative example — it shows the level of specificity, not a voice to copy.)

The Hard Rules

These keep your voice distinct, consistent, and actually usable — and the Builder holds you to them.

True over safe. Real attributes with flavor, never "professional/approachable/trustworthy." At least one with an edge.

Concrete over abstract. Actual words, actual rules, actual examples — not categories and vibes.

Yours, not borrowed. Built from how you actually talk, never a guru's voice stapled on.

Tone flexes, identity doesn't. Sound different when you sell vs support, but always sound like you.

Keep your signature phrase. The thing you always say is gold — protect it, don't polish it out.

Set the mechanics. Reading level, paragraph length, punctuation, emojis — spelled out, especially for AI.

Show, don't just tell. At least three before/after pairs you recognize as yours.

The copy test. A stranger or an AI could write in your voice from this guide. If not, get more concrete.

Common Mistakes & Fixes

Almost every generic-sounding brand falls into one of these.

1. The "professional yet approachable" trap

What it looks like: voice attributes of "professional, approachable, trustworthy."

Why it happens: those words feel safe and unarguable, so everyone lands on them.

Why it costs you: they describe every brand, so they make yours sound like all the others.

The fix: push for the flavor only you have, with at least one word that has an edge.

2. Adjectives with nothing under them

What it looks like: three nice words and no word lists, rules, or examples.

Why it happens: naming adjectives feels like finishing, so people stop there.

Why it costs you: you can't write from adjectives — the copy stays generic despite the label.

The fix: add the concrete layers: power words, rules, before/afters, and a sample.

3. Borrowing a voice

What it looks like: copying a big creator's punchy style because it works for them.

Why it happens: their voice is proven, so it feels like a safe bet.

Why it costs you: a borrowed voice doesn't fit, so you can't keep it up and it rings false.

The fix: start from a sample of your own writing. Sharpen how you already sound.

4. One tone for everything

What it looks like: the same energy whether you're selling or consoling.

Why it happens: "consistency" gets mistaken for "identical everywhere."

Why it costs you: a hard-sell tone on a supportive message pushes people away.

The fix: define how the tone flexes across selling, teaching, supporting, and celebrating.

5. Skipping the mechanics

What it looks like: no rules on paragraph length, punctuation, or emojis.

Why it happens: the mechanical stuff feels boring and unimportant.

Why it costs you: without rules, AI-written copy drifts and your formatting is inconsistent.

The fix: set the mechanics once. It's what makes tools match you exactly.

6. No sample to copy

What it looks like: a voice guide with rules but no example paragraph.

Why it happens: writing a full sample feels like extra work at the end.

Why it costs you: the sample is the most-used part — without it, people still guess.

The fix: write one short paragraph that's unmistakably you, and make it the reference.

Is It Ready?

This foundation isn't scored out of 25 — a voice is judged by whether it's distinct, consistent, concrete, and truly yours. So it passes a Ready Check: every item a clear yes.

  • You have 3–5 specific, true voice attributes — not the safe defaults — each with a "means / doesn't mean" line.
  • At least one attribute has an edge.
  • Your tone flexes across selling, teaching, supporting, and celebrating — and still fits your voice.
  • Your signature style names real habits, including any signature phrase.
  • You have a real power-words list (8–15) and a real banned list — actual words.
  • Your grammar and formatting rules are set — reading level, paragraphs, punctuation, emojis.
  • You have at least three before/after pairs you recognize as yours.
  • You have one gold-standard sample paragraph that's unmistakably you.
  • The copy test: a stranger or an AI could write in your voice from this guide.

All boxes checked? Your Brand Voice is locked — and that's all three Brand Foundations done. You know who you are, how you look, and how you sound. Time to build the Formula.
