The 3×3 Structure

Every step is a "mini promise"

The whole system solves the big problem — your $Million Promise™.

Each stage solves a major piece of it. Each step solves one smaller problem and delivers one quick win.

That's why every step can become content on its own: a post, an email, a workshop, a lead magnet.

The timeline has to match

Your 9 steps must realistically deliver your $Million Promise™ inside the promise's timeline.

If your promise says "in 90 days," the nine steps need to add up to 90 days or less.

✔ The quick check

Add up the honest time for all 9 steps.

If it blows past your promise timeline, tighten the steps or fix the timeline — don't pretend the gap isn't there.

Know Your Business Type

Before you write a single outcome, you need to know what kind of business you have.

It decides how your step outcomes are written and what language is allowed.

Type 1

Product Creator

You teach or build; the client implements. Coaches, consultants, course creators, memberships, done-for-you.

Type 2

Journey Professional

You guide a client through a high-stakes transition — you do it with or for them. Attorneys, medical, financial advisors.

Type 3

Hybrid

You do both — teach AND guide through a defined transition. Career coaches, test prep, condition-specific health coaches.

Here's the fast way to tell which you are.

Ask: is my client packaging expertise into something they implement — or am I guiding them through a high-stakes transition?

If you say "I teach them how to…" you're a Product Creator. If you say "I guide them through…" or "I represent them in…" you're a Journey Professional.

Forcing Product Creator rules onto a Journey Professional.

A divorce attorney doesn't hand the client "an asset in a folder" at every step — they reach milestones and protect positions.

Force the wrong framework and you invent fake deliverables that don't match how the business really works.

Naming Your System

Your Magic Formula™ needs a name — the thing people repeat when they tell a friend about you.

Without it, you're "a coach who helps with X." With it, you're the creator of a system.

Start with the name you discovered in your $Million Moment™.

That "[Name] System" is your seed — sometimes it's already the final name, sometimes it points you to a stronger one.

Avoid commoditized words

For smart buyers, words like "webinar," "funnel," "course," and "program" feel generic.

Name the OUTCOME, not the format.

✕ Commoditized

"Webinar Launch System"

"Client Funnel Program"

"Divorce Support Course"

✓ Outcome-focused

"The 90-Minute Sales Machine"

"The Booked-Out Blueprint"

"The Strategic Divorce Method"

Formats that work

Pick the one that fits your style: The [Name] Method, System, Formula, Machine, Engine, Protocol (strong for Journey Professionals), or Blueprint.

Then write the subtitle

Your subtitle is your $Million Promise™ turned into marketing copy.

Don't use "I help [avatar]…" — that's how you define the offer, not how you sell it.

Use "How [avatar] [outcome] — without [obstacles]."

✔ Name + subtitle, together

The 90-Minute Sales Machine™

How coaches launch a high-converting webinar in 90 minutes — without blank screens or slide decks.

Stage Naming

Your three stages need names that are consistent, memorable, and hint at the progression.

The taxonomy rule

Stage names must be all verbs OR all nouns — never mixed.

Mixing them feels off, even if the reader can't say why.

✕ Mixed (feels off)

Stage 1: Foundation (noun)

Stage 2: Build (verb)

Stage 3: Success (noun)

✓ Consistent (all nouns)

Stage 1: Foundation

Stage 2: Framework

Stage 3: Fuel

Alliteration is optional but powerful — stages that start with the same letter stick.

"Message, Machine, Momentum" or "Design, Develop, Deploy" are easy to remember and repeat.

Good step names are short (2–4 words), intriguing, benefit-hinting, and brandable.

✔ The click test

Would someone click on this step name if it were a free lead magnet?

If not, make it more specific and more intriguing.

Step Outcomes by Business Type

Every step needs an outcome — the specific win at the end.

What counts as a valid outcome depends on your business type.

Product Creator — "What do they HAVE at the end?"

Assets built: documents, frameworks, scripts, plans, checklists.

Shorter than the whole system — minutes, hours, or days — and it feels like a real quick win.

Journey Professional — "What is TRUE now that wasn't before?"

Milestones reached, decisions made, or positions established.

"We" language is correct here — you ARE doing it with them.

"Feel confident," "feel ready," "feel protected" — blocked for every business type.

Ask what they'd HAVE or what would be TRUE that makes them feel that way, and write that instead.

The Hot Step → Your Event Magnet™

You don't need the whole system live to start pulling people in.

That's your hot step. We flag it here as the seed of your Event Magnet™.

Your Event Magnet™ is the free lead magnet built from that hot step — its own tool, further down the road.

Down the line it fills your paid events — that's Pillar 2, your Money Magnet™.

✔ Don't confuse the two

Money Magnet™ is a whole pillar (Pillar 2) — how you turn attention into cash.

Event Magnet™ is the single free lead magnet you build from one hot step to feed it.

In this tool, you're only picking the hot step. You're not building either one yet.

What makes a strong hot step

  • One clear win — it solves one specific problem from your system.
  • Teachable fast — you could walk someone through it in a short video or PDF.
  • Quick to consume — real value in about 10 minutes or one sitting.
  • Incomplete by design — it solves one piece and leaves them wanting the full system.
  • Obvious next step — the path to your paid offer is clear once they've used it.

Avoid the obvious, boring, foundational steps — nobody opts in for "Define your avatar."

The best hot steps usually come from Stage 1 or early Stage 2, where the first "aha" lives.

Common Mistakes & Fixes

1. Feeling outcomes instead of real wins

What it looks like: "Feel confident about your pricing." "Feel ready to launch."

Why it happens: Feelings are easy to write and sound nice, so they slip in when a step is fuzzy.

Why it costs you: Feelings can't be proven, pictured, or sold — clients buy results, not moods.

The fix: Ask what they'd HAVE or what would be TRUE that creates the feeling. "Your pricing locked in — you know exactly what to charge."

2. Boring step names

What it looks like: "Define your avatar." "Create your outline." "Build your offer."

Why it happens: You're describing the task instead of the payoff.

Why it costs you: Homework-style names kill curiosity, and a step nobody wants can't become a lead magnet.

The fix: Make the name hint at the win and create curiosity. "Define your avatar" → "The Bullseye Buyer."

3. Mixed stage taxonomy

What it looks like: Stage 1: Foundation, Stage 2: Build, Stage 3: Success — noun, verb, noun.

Why it happens: You name each stage on its own instead of as a set.

Why it costs you: It feels disjointed and slightly unprofessional, even if nobody can name why.

The fix: Pick all nouns or all verbs. "Foundation, Framework, Fuel" or "Design, Develop, Deploy."

4. The timeline doesn't match the promise

What it looks like: Your promise says "90 days," but the nine steps would really take six months.

Why it happens: You build the system without checking it against the clock you already promised.

Why it costs you: You're making a promise you can't keep — the client fails, or your promise was a lie.

The fix: Add up the honest time for all 9 steps. If it's over, tighten the steps or fix the timeline.

5. Forcing the wrong business type

What it looks like: Asking a divorce attorney "what asset does the client have in a folder?" for every step.

Why it happens: You apply Product Creator rules to a Journey Professional out of habit.

Why it costs you: You invent fake deliverables that don't match how the business really works.

The fix: Classify your type first. Use milestones, decisions, and positions for Journey Professionals.

6. Confusing the hot step with the Money Magnet™

What it looks like: Calling the one hot step "my Money Magnet™" and trying to build it here.

Why it happens: The names are close, and the hot step really does feed the Money Magnet™ pillar.

Why it costs you: You blur two different things and try to build a pillar during a system-building step.

The fix: The hot step seeds your Event Magnet™ (the free lead magnet). Money Magnet™ is the whole Pillar 2, built later.
