What Is the Golden Avatar™?

Your Golden Avatar™ is the one real buyer your whole promise is built for — profiled in enough depth that you know exactly who to target and what to say.

Your $Million Promise™ named WHO you help in a few words.

The Golden Avatar™ takes that name and goes deep.

Who this person really is. Where they're stuck right now. What they've already tried that failed.

The one outcome they'd pay for today, the objections that stop them, the exact places they hang out, and the way they actually talk about all of it.

It's not a made-up character with a stock photo. It's a portrait of a real person you already serve, built from real evidence.

Most of the time you'll build just the Golden Avatar™.

Sometimes your market is too small to run ads to one buyer alone, and you widen out to one or two overlapping buyers who want the same result.

But you only do that when the numbers say you have to.

This guide covers both: how to build one avatar deeply, and how to decide whether your market needs more than one.

It matters because everything you do to reach people runs on this.

Your ad targeting is only as good as your picture of who to target and where to find them.

Your content only lands if it's written to one specific person with one specific pain.

Your sales calls only feel easy when the prospect shows up already feeling understood.

When your avatar is real and specific, the platform finds your buyer cheaply, your posts get the "are you inside my head?" reaction, and closing feels like agreeing instead of convincing.

That's the whole return on this hour of work.

And when your avatar is vague or invented, the opposite happens — quietly and expensively.

You target "small business owners" and the ad reaches millions of the wrong ones.

You write content for "everyone" and it lands on no one.

You get on calls where you're guessing at objections because you never actually asked.

Most people who think they have a targeting problem or a content problem really have an avatar problem — they never pinned down the real human, so every message downstream is aimed at a ghost.

What the Golden Avatar™ is NOT

It helps to be just as clear about what this isn't, because most people build the wrong thing and wonder why it doesn't help.

It's not a made-up character — the "Marketing Mary, age 34, likes yoga and podcasts" stock persona is a fiction exercise that feels productive and targets no one.

It's not a demographic slice on its own — age, income, and job title tell an ad platform who to show the ad to, but they say nothing about what makes the person stop and care.

It's not your best-case dream client you'd love to attract — it's a portrait of who you actually serve now.

And it's not one giant "everyone I could help" blob — the moment you try to describe your whole market as one person, you lose the specificity that made the profile useful.

Here's a simple gut-check: if you couldn't point to a real call, review, or client behind a line in your profile, that line isn't research — it's a guess. And guesses are exactly what this tool exists to catch.

First: How Many Avatars Do You Need?

Before you profile anyone, you answer one question — and it's a math question, not a matter of taste.

How many avatars does your market actually need?

The answer is driven entirely by market size, and it follows the same sizing rule as your $Million Promise™.

The target is a reachable market big enough to run paid ads to — roughly 5 million+ people for a B2C offer, or about 1 million+ businesses for B2B.

Here's how the count gets decided:

  • Your Golden Avatar™ — the exact buyer your promise names — always comes first.
  • If that one audience is already big enough (5M+ / 1M+), one avatar is all you need. A single, sharp avatar is the strongest thing you can have.
  • If your bullseye is smaller than that, you overlap — you add one or two related circles who want the same core outcome, until the combined, reachable market clears the bar.
  • You never pad the number with unrelated audiences just to hit three. Overlap only — same outcome, different flavor of person.

Why size drives the count

If you run ads to a market that's too small, the platform runs out of people to show your ad to, costs climb, and results stall — no matter how good your avatar is.

Widening to an overlapping avatar is how you grow the reachable pool without losing the shared message that makes your marketing work.

But widening for any other reason — because you "could help" more kinds of people — just blurs your message.

So the rule is simple: add an avatar only when the math forces it, and only if it truly overlaps.

Overlapping (concentric) vs. unrelated

Overlapping avatars share a Core Currency — they all want the same result, just from a slightly different starting point.

Course creators, coaches, and consultants can overlap because they all want more paying clients: same outcome, different person.

But busy parents, single professionals, and retirees do not overlap — they want different things out of life, so they'd buy different offers and need different promises.

Three unrelated avatars isn't a bigger audience; it's three different businesses wearing one coat, and it will pull your marketing in three directions at once.

The test before adding an avatar: does this next person want the same core outcome as my Golden Avatar™, just in a different situation?

If yes, overlap them. If no, stop — that's a different business for a different day.

The builder walks you through this decision first.

It sizes your market with you — it will ask for your estimate, and it never makes a number up — and only then starts building.

Most coaches land on one or two. Three is the ceiling, not the goal.

How You'll Build It

Once you know how many avatars you're building, you build each one the same way.

Not by imagining a person and writing down what you assume — but by pulling from evidence you already have: sales calls, reviews, DMs, surveys, real clients.

You organize that evidence into seven fields, one avatar at a time.

This is exactly what the Golden Avatar™ Builder walks you through, but seeing the whole shape first makes the build faster and far more honest.

For each avatar you fill in seven fields in order: Snapshot & Label, Situation & Demographics, Pains & Failed Attempts, Desires & the One Outcome, Objections & Beliefs, Watering Holes, and Voice of Customer.

In the builder you can answer one field for one avatar at a time, or — if you already know a field for all of them — give them all at once.

Either way, every avatar has to pass a score before it locks (more on that at the end).

The rest of this guide teaches each of those seven fields in full — what it is, why it matters, how to find yours from real evidence, the right and wrong versions, and the exact trap that quietly turns a profile into fiction.

The 7 Fields, In Full

Field 1

Snapshot & Label — name the person

The Snapshot is a short, memorable label plus one line on who this person is — the way you'd describe them to a friend over coffee.

"Second-Year Sarah — a real estate agent two years in, closing a few deals but stuck under six figures."

A label and a one-line situation, and suddenly you can picture a real human instead of a category.

Why it matters. A name makes the avatar usable.

When you sit down to write an ad or a post, "who am I talking to?" has a one-word answer you can hold in your head — Sarah — instead of a fuzzy demographic cloud.

The label is a handle for everything else in the profile, and the one-line situation forces you to commit to a specific person at a specific stage. That's where all the useful specificity comes from.

How to find yours

Think of a real client or a real pattern of clients — not your dream buyer, the actual people who've paid you or shown up on calls.

Give them a name that captures their stage or their defining trait, then one line on where they are right now.

Keep the label human and slightly sticky so it's easy to remember.

The test: could you introduce this person to a colleague in one sentence and have them instantly picture who you mean?

✓ Do this

"Second-Year Sarah — real estate agent, 2 years in, closing a few deals, stuck under six figures" · "Burnt-Out Ben — solo bookkeeper with 12 clients, working nights, can't take on more without drowning"

✕ Not that

"Busy professional" · "My ideal client" · "Small business owner" · "Marketing Mary, 34, loves yoga" (a stock caricature with no real basis)

Field 2

Situation & Demographics — where they really are

This field paints their real situation — both the practical facts and where their head is at.

The facts: rough age range, their role or title, whether they're B2B or B2C, their income level, solo or with a team, the tools they use, how busy they are.

The situation: where they are in their journey right now — just starting, plateaued, or scaling — and what a normal week actually looks like for them.

Why it matters. The two halves do two different jobs, and you need both.

The demographic facts tell an ad platform who to show your message to — age, role, industry, location are literally the targeting inputs.

But the situation is what makes the message land once they see it.

Two people with the same job title but at different stages — one just starting, one plateaued — have completely different pains and buy completely different things.

Demographics without situation is a mailing list. Situation without demographics is a feeling you can't target.

Together, they're a person you can find AND move.

How to find yours

Pull from the real clients you've served, not a stereotype of your market.

Look at who actually books calls and buys — their real ages, real roles, real stage of business.

If you catch yourself writing what you assume is true rather than what you've seen, mark it to verify and go check it against a few real people.

And keep the situation tied to the outcome your promise sells: the stage that matters most is the one where your offer is the obvious next move.

✓ Do this

"35–45, real estate agent, 2–4 years in, ~$70K/yr, solo, uses a basic CRM, works 6 days a week, plateaued and frustrated"

✕ Not that

"25–65, anyone in business, wants to grow" (no stage, no reality) · a pure demographic list with no sense of where they're stuck

Field 3

Pains & Failed Attempts — what hurts, and what flopped

This is the field that makes a profile powerful.

Two things, aimed at the same target: the pain that keeps this person up at night around your core outcome, and what they've already tried to fix it that failed — a course, a coach, a tool, a tactic, a hire.

Not how they feel. What they did, and how it flopped.

Why it matters. People don't buy because they hurt; they buy because they've tried to fix the hurt and it didn't work.

When your marketing names the exact thing they tried and hated — "you hired an agency that ghosted you," "you posted every day and got crickets" — something clicks: this person actually gets my situation.

That recognition is what separates content people scroll past from content that stops them.

And it's the field people most often fake, because failed attempts require you to have actually asked. This is where you find out whether you know your customer or just imagine them.

A pain is a failed attempt, not a feeling

This is the same rule that governs the pitfall in your $Million Promise™, and it's just as important here.

"Overwhelmed," "stressed," "not confident," "afraid of wasting money" — those are feelings happening to the person, not things they tried.

Naming a feeling proves nothing; naming the failed attempt behind it proves you understand their world.

The move is always the same: take the feeling and ask, "what did they actually do about this that didn't work?"

Overwhelmed becomes "tried to DIY it from YouTube and gave up." Afraid of wasting money becomes "already burned $3K on a course they never finished."

Turn every feeling into the failed attempt underneath it.

Where to find the real ones

Go to your evidence, not your imagination.

Sales calls are the goldmine — people describe exactly what they tried when they explain why they're talking to you.

Reviews and testimonials often open with the failed path ("I'd tried three other programs before this one"). DMs and survey answers are full of it.

Collect the two or three most painful, keep them tied to your core outcome, and use their words, not yours.

✓ Do this

"Hired a VA who made it worse" · "Bought a $2K course, watched two modules, quit" · "Ran boosted posts for months and got nothing" · "Tried cold DMs till their account got flagged"

✕ Not that

"Overwhelmed" · "Stuck" · "Lacks confidence" · "Afraid it won't work" (feelings, not attempts) · "They've tried everything" (name two specifics)

Field 4

Desires & the One Outcome — what they'd pay for today

Flip the pain over.

This field captures what they want — a few desires, then the ONE outcome they'd pay for right now.

And that one outcome has to line up with the Core Currency from your $Million Promise™.

If your promise sells "your first 50 paying students," the one outcome here is a version of that same thing, in this avatar's words.

Why it matters. A profile with one clear desired outcome is a laser; a profile with five competing desires is a fog.

The one outcome is what your offer promises to deliver and what all your messaging points toward.

Keeping it matched to your promise's currency is what keeps the whole $Million Story™ coherent — the promise, the avatar, and eventually the offer all point at the same finish line.

When the avatar's real desire doesn't match your promise, that's not a small thing to smooth over. It's a signal that either this isn't your bullseye or your promise needs another look.

Pick the one they'd pay for, not the one you're proudest of

Coaches love to lead with the outcome they find most meaningful — the deep transformation, the mindset shift.

But the one outcome here is the one that makes the customer lean forward and reach for a card.

Often that's more concrete and more selfish than you'd like: more clients, more revenue, more time, a specific number hit by a specific date.

Sell the outcome they'd pay for today, and deliver the deeper transformation once they're in.

Keep it out of the guru zone

The same trap from the promise applies: "freedom," "transformation," "confidence," and "clarity" can't be counted, so they can't anchor a profile.

Trade the abstract word for the concrete result underneath it, and make sure it matches the measurable currency your promise already committed to.

If you can't measure it, you can't sell it — and you definitely can't build content and offers around it.

✓ Do this

"Their first 50 paying students" · "5 new listings a month" · "$10K months without working weekends" — one clear outcome, matched to the promise

✕ Not that

"Freedom" · "Confidence" · "To finally feel successful" (guru zone) · three different end goals at once · an outcome that doesn't match the promise's currency

Field 5

Objections & Beliefs — the brakes on the sale

This field captures why this person hesitates when they're this close to buying.

Their real objections: "I don't have time," "I've been burned before," "this won't work in my niche," "I can't afford it right now."

Plus the limiting beliefs they hold that get in the way: "you need a big audience to launch," "ads don't work for someone like me."

Objections are what they say out loud. Beliefs are the false things underneath that make them say it.

Why it matters. Every objection you can name in advance is one your marketing gets to answer before the prospect even raises it — in an ad, an email, a piece of content, a sales script.

Marketing that ignores objections leaves the prospect to talk themselves out of buying in silence.

And the limiting beliefs are even more valuable: a false belief is the thing your content gets to break, and breaking it is often what turns a follower into a buyer.

If your avatar believes "you need a huge audience to launch," a single post that shatters that belief can do more than ten posts about your offer.

Price is usually the last mask

When someone says "I can't afford it," that's often the polite version of a deeper fear.

That it won't work for them specifically. That they'll fail again like they did last time. That they're not the kind of person this works for.

Don't stop at the surface objection. Dig for the real fear underneath, because that's the one your marketing actually has to release.

"I can't afford it" gets answered with a payment plan. "I've failed at this before and I'm scared to try again" gets answered with proof, empathy, and a story — which is far more powerful.

Find them in real conversations, not your head

You cannot guess objections accurately — you have to have heard them.

They live in the second half of sales calls, in the "I'm not sure because…" emails, in the reasons people give when they say no.

If you don't have real objections yet, that's your homework: get on calls and listen for the moment they hesitate.

An invented objection sends your marketing after a fear nobody actually has.

✓ Do this

Objection: "I've bought courses before and never finished them." Belief: "I need a big following before ads make sense." — both pulled from real calls

✕ Not that

"They'd probably say it's too expensive" (a guess) · only surface price objections with no deeper fear · no limiting belief at all

Field 6

Watering Holes — exactly where they gather

Watering holes are the specific places this person actually hangs out — the ones you could point an ad or a piece of content at.

Not "social media." Which platform. Which groups.

Which creators they follow, podcasts they play, tools they log into, events they attend, newsletters they open.

Names, not categories.

Why it matters. This is the field that turns a profile into a targeting plan.

Knowing your avatar deeply is useless if you don't know where to find them — and "online" isn't an answer, because online is everywhere, which is nowhere.

Specific watering holes tell you exactly which interests to target in ad manager, which creators to get in front of, which podcasts to pitch, which communities to show up in.

The more specific and named your list, the cheaper and sharper your reach — because you're fishing where the fish already are instead of dragging a net across the whole ocean.

Cold water, not just warm

It's tempting to list your own email list and followers — but those are people who already know you.

The watering holes that matter most are where the cold ones gather, the people who've never heard your name.

Those are the pools ads and content have to reach to actually grow.

Push past your owned channels to the places your future buyers already spend time without you.

If you don't know, that's the assignment

Not knowing where your avatar hangs out isn't a gap to paper over with a guess — it's a research task.

The fastest fix is to ask five real people in your market a single question: "where do you go to learn about this?"

Their answers — the specific shows, groups, and people they name — become your targeting map.

A guessed watering hole wastes ad spend pointing at an empty room.

✓ Do this

"The [named] Facebook group for new agents" · "[Specific] real estate podcast" · "Follows [named creator] on Instagram" · "Uses [named CRM]" · "Attends [named] annual conference"

✕ Not that

"Social media" · "Online" · "Google" · "LinkedIn" (which part? whose content? which groups?) · only "my own email list" (that's warm, not cold)

Field 7

Voice of Customer — their real words

The last field is the most valuable one you'll bank: 3–5 real quotes from this person, word for word, the way they said it.

Straight from a call, a review, a DM, a survey. Unpolished.

"I feel like I'm working 60 hours a week and I'm still broke" — not "struggles with work-life balance and income."

Why it matters. The single fastest way to write copy that converts is to use the customer's own language back to them.

When an ad or email uses the exact words a prospect uses in their own head, it slips past their skepticism and feels like recognition instead of selling.

This field is a swipe file you'll reuse for the life of your business — headlines, ad hooks, email subject lines, sales-page bullets all get easier when you're pulling from real quotes instead of inventing clever lines.

Copywriters pay for this language; you're collecting it straight from the source.

Don't clean it up

The power is in the mess.

Real people talk in run-ons, in specifics, in slightly-off phrasing that a marketer would never write — and that's exactly why it works, because it sounds human.

The moment you "professionalize" a quote into "struggles with financial organization," you strip out the thing that made it land.

Keep their grammar, their word choices, their emphasis. If it makes you wince a little because it's so raw, it's perfect.

Real only — never invented

This field is only powerful when it's genuine.

A quote you wrote yourself, imagining how a customer might talk, is just your own copy wearing a costume — and it usually shows.

If you don't have real quotes yet, don't fake them; mark the field as something to collect and go get three real ones from calls or reviews.

Three genuine quotes beat ten invented ones every time.

✓ Do this

"I'm doing everything the gurus say and my account's still a ghost town" · "I've spent more on courses than I've made back" · "I just want ONE month where I'm not scared to check my bank balance"

✕ Not that

"Struggles with lead generation" · "Desires financial stability" (you wrote that, not them) · invented quotes with no real source · fewer than three

The Hard Rules

These aren't preferences — they're the guardrails that keep a profile honest and usable, and the Golden Avatar™ Builder enforces every one.

Here's each rule and the reason behind it.

Market size decides the count. You build as many avatars as it takes to reach a viable market (~5M+ people or ~1M+ businesses) — and no more. One is ideal if the bullseye already clears the bar; you overlap only when the numbers force it.

Evidence, not imagination. Every field must come from a real source — a call, review, DM, survey, or client you've served. Anything you can't trace to real evidence gets flagged UNVERIFIED, because a made-up avatar is worse than none.

Avatar #1 is the person your promise names. Your Golden Avatar™ is fixed by Part 1. You don't get to quietly swap in a different, easier-to-imagine person here.

Three avatars maximum, and only if concentric. Any avatar beyond your Golden Avatar™ must genuinely overlap and share the same core outcome. Unrelated avatars mean unrelated promises and scattered marketing.

Pains are failed attempts, not feelings. "Overwhelmed" is a feeling; "hired an agency that ghosted them" is a failed attempt. Only the failed attempt proves you understand their world.

One outcome per avatar, matched to the promise. Each avatar points at a single result they'd pay for, and it must be the same currency your $Million Promise™ sells. A mismatch means either the avatar or the promise is off.

Watering holes must be specific and named. "Social media" is not a targeting plan. Name the exact groups, shows, creators, tools, and events — or go collect them.

Voice-of-customer stays in their real words. Never polish a customer quote into consultant-speak. The raw, unpolished language is the whole point — it's what makes copy convert.

Every avatar must pass the score to lock. A profile isn't finished until it scores 18/25 or higher with a 3+ on every field. Weak or guessed profiles don't advance — that's the gate, explained in full below.

Common Mistakes & Fixes

Almost every weak avatar fails in one of the ways below.

For each, here's what it looks like, why it happens, why it quietly costs you money, and exactly how to fix it.

1. Inventing the customer instead of researching them

This is the big one — sitting down and imagining your ideal client, writing down what you assume they think, want, and fear.

It happens because it's fast and it feels productive; you can fill in all seven fields in ten minutes without talking to anyone.

But an invented avatar is a fiction, and marketing built on fiction points at a person who doesn't exist.

You'll target the wrong interests, write to the wrong pains, and answer objections nobody actually has — and you won't know it's wrong until the ad spend is gone.

Fix: treat every field as a research question, not a creative-writing prompt. Pull from real calls, reviews, DMs, and surveys. If you don't have the evidence yet, talk to five real people in your market before you build — and mark anything unverified so you know exactly what to go prove.

2. Building three avatars when one would do

This looks like profiling three different groups because you've helped all of them, when your bullseye market is already plenty big.

It happens because more avatars feels like more opportunity, and turning anyone away feels like leaving money on the table.

But every extra avatar splits your message and your ad budget, and if your first audience was already viable, the extras just dilute a profile that was working.

You end up vaguer to everyone in an effort to include everyone.

Fix: size your market first. If your Golden Avatar™'s audience already clears ~5M people (or ~1M businesses), build that one deeply and stop. Add an overlapping avatar only when the math forces it — not because you could help more kinds of people.

3. Demographics with no situation

This looks like "35–50, business owner, household income $100K+" and nothing else.

It happens because demographics are easy to state and feel concrete.

But age and income tell you who to show an ad to, not what makes them stop and care.

Two people with identical demographics at different stages of their journey have completely different pains and buy completely different things, so a profile that stops at demographics can't tell you what to actually say.

Fix: add the stage and the daily reality. Where are they stuck right now — starting, plateaued, scaling? What does a normal week look like? The situation is what turns a mailing list into a person you can actually move.

4. Feelings instead of failed attempts

This is filling the pain field with "overwhelmed," "stressed," "lacks confidence," "afraid to invest."

It happens because those feelings are real and top-of-mind.

But a feeling is something happening to the person, not something they tried — and naming a feeling doesn't prove you understand their situation the way naming a failed attempt does.

"You feel overwhelmed" could be said to anyone; "you tried to DIY it from YouTube and gave up" could only be said to someone you actually get.

Fix: take each feeling and ask "what did they actually do about this that didn't work?" Turn "overwhelmed" into "bought three courses and finished none," and name that instead.

5. Too many outcomes, or a guru-zone one

This shows up as an avatar who wants "freedom, confidence, more clients, and better systems" all at once, or whose one desire is an abstract like "transformation."

It happens because your clients genuinely want many things and you don't want to leave any out.

But a profile with five competing desires can't tell you what to lead with, and a guru-zone desire can't be measured, targeted, or sold.

The result is messaging that tries to promise everything and lands on nothing.

Fix: pick the ONE outcome they'd pay for today, make it concrete and countable, and match it to your promise's Core Currency. The other desires become bonuses you deliver once they're in — not the hook.

6. Guessing objections instead of hearing them

This is writing "they'd probably think it's too expensive" without ever having had someone say it.

It happens because you can imagine objections without doing the work of collecting them.

But guessed objections send your marketing after fears nobody actually has, while the real objection — the one that's actually killing your sales — goes unanswered.

And a stopped-at-price objection usually hides a deeper fear you never surfaced.

Fix: get the real objections from the second half of sales calls and the reasons people give when they say no. Dig past "I can't afford it" to the real fear underneath — usually "it won't work for someone like me." Answer that.

7. Vague watering holes

This is answering "where do they hang out?" with "social media," "online," or "LinkedIn."

It happens because the broad answer feels true — they probably are on social media.

But broad is useless for targeting: you can't run an ad to "online," and "LinkedIn" doesn't tell you whose content they read or which groups they're in.

A vague watering hole is the same as not knowing, and it turns ad targeting back into guesswork.

Fix: demand names — the specific groups, podcasts, creators, tools, and events. If you don't know them, ask five real people "where do you go to learn about this?" and use their exact answers as your targeting map.

8. Polishing the customer's words

This is turning "I'm working 60 hours a week and still broke" into "struggles with work-life balance and income."

It happens because the polished version looks more professional and you're trained to write cleanly.

But polishing strips out the raw, specific language that made the quote land — the messy realness is exactly what slips past a prospect's skepticism.

A cleaned-up quote reads like marketing; the raw one reads like recognition.

Fix: keep every quote word-for-word, grammar and all. If it makes you wince because it's so raw, it's perfect. Bank 3–5 real ones per avatar and reuse them as your swipe file forever.

Score Your Own — The Pass Bar

The Golden Avatar™ Builder doesn't just score your work — it gates on it.

An avatar won't lock, and you won't move on to the next step, until it earns a passing score.

Scoring each avatar by hand before you run the builder tells you exactly where the weak spots are.

Here's the whole system in three simple pieces.

1. Each field is scored 0–5

Every avatar is judged on five fields. Each field gets a score from 0 to 5.

Here's what each number means, and what good versus bad looks like:

ScoreWhat it meansWhat it looks like
5Nailed itSpecific, backed by real evidence, in the customer's own words, usable in an ad today.
4StrongSpecific and real, with only minor sharpening left to do.
3Passable — the floorIdentifiable but still broad, or partly assumed. The minimum a single field can score and still count.
2WeakVague, feeling-based, or a category ("business owners") instead of a specific person or place.
1FailGeneric, invented, or "everyone." No real evidence behind it.
0MissingNot there at all.

2. Five fields, 25 points

The five scored fields add up to 25.

(Your Snapshot label and your voice-of-customer quotes feed these fields — they sharpen the scores rather than getting a score of their own.)

FieldA 5 looks likeA 2 looks like

3. The pass bar

Add up the five fields. Where your total lands tells you what to do next:

18–25 🔒 Locked

Green light. Evidence-backed and specific. This avatar passes — move on to the next step.

13–17 Almost

Real but soft. Raise every field that's under 3, then push your weakest fields up until you clear 18.

0–12 Rebuild

You're likely guessing. Go get real evidence — calls, reviews, DMs — before you build any further.

✔ The hard rule

To lock, an avatar needs 18 out of 25 or higher AND a 3 or better on every single field. Both conditions, not one.

A single field sitting at a 2 fails the whole avatar even if the total looks fine — because one weak spot is where your marketing will leak.

The builder won't advance you to your $Million Moment™ until every avatar you're building clears this bar.

And here's the honest part: any field you can't back with real evidence gets capped at a 3, so a profile full of guesses simply can't reach 18. That's by design — it's what stops you from spending real money on a made-up customer.

The quick checklist

Run each avatar against this before you call it done:

  • Avatar #1 is the exact audience your $Million Promise™ names — your Golden Avatar™.
  • All seven fields are filled — Snapshot & Label, Situation & Demographics, Pains & Failed Attempts, Desires & the One Outcome, Objections & Beliefs, Watering Holes, and Voice of Customer.
  • Every field is backed by real evidence — calls, reviews, DMs, surveys — not guesses.
  • The pains are real failed attempts, and the desires land on ONE outcome, not a wish list.
  • The Voice of Customer is in their actual words, not your paraphrase.
  • The watering holes are specific places you can actually reach them.
  • If you built more than one avatar, they're concentric — related, overlapping circles — and you've capped it at three.
  • Every avatar scores 18 out of 25 or higher, with a 3 or better on every scored field.
  • No field is propped up by a guess — anything you can't back with evidence is capped at a 3.

All boxes checked on every avatar? Your Golden Avatar™ is locked — and now your marketing can sound like it was written from inside their head.
