Brand Foundations · Foundation 2 of 3

Turn your brand personality into a simple, consistent look — the colors, fonts, and rules that make everything you publish feel like one brand.

What Is the Visual Style Guide?

Your Visual Style Guide is the small set of rules that makes everything you publish look like it came from the same place.

It's your colors, your fonts, your logo, and the way your images and buttons look — written down so you use them the same way every time.

Not a mood board. A working system a non-designer can actually keep.

It matters because consistency is what makes a brand feel real.

When your site, your slides, your posts, and your ads all share the same hero color and the same fonts, people start to recognize you.

That recognition builds trust, and trust is what makes strangers comfortable enough to buy.

When your look is all over the place — a different color here, a random font there — you look smaller, newer, and less trustworthy than you actually are.

Here's the thing most people get backwards.

A strong brand isn't about having more — more colors, more fonts, more effects.

It's about having a few good choices and repeating them everywhere. Discipline is the whole game.

1
One hero color. The single most important rule in this guide. One signature color, used consistently, does more for recognition than a rainbow ever will.

And your look isn't random — it comes straight from who you are.

That's why this is Foundation 2, built right after your Business Brand Profile. Your personality picks your palette; your positioning shapes your feel.

What the Visual Style Guide is NOT

It's not a logo. A logo is one piece of it — and you don't even need a fancy one to start; a clean wordmark works.

It's not artwork or a finished website. It's the rules those things follow, written in plain text you can hand to a designer or a tool.

It's not about chasing design trends. Trends age; a consistent system compounds.

And it's not your Brand Voice — that's Foundation 3, how you sound. This is only how you look.

How You'll Build It

You build a visual system in seven small moves, each one a decision you lock and reuse forever.

The Builder walks you through them one at a time, always tied back to your personality.

Here's the path: name the feeling your look should give, choose your color (one hero plus neutrals), set your typography (two fonts), document your logo or wordmark, define your imagery, lock your components (buttons, headings, callouts), and write your consistency rules.

The rest of this guide teaches each move in full — what it is, why it matters, how to choose yours, and the trap that quietly makes brands look messy.

One feeling → one hero color + two fonts + one consistent look, everywhere.

The 7 Moves, In Full

1

Your Look in a Word — the feeling

Before you pick anything, you name the feeling your visuals should give — bold, warm, premium, playful, clean.

Why it matters. The feeling is the filter every other choice passes through. Without it, you're picking colors and fonts on vibes, and vibes drift.

How to choose yours

Pull it straight from your personality. A Challenger brand feels bold and high-contrast; a Friend brand feels warm and welcoming; an Expert brand feels premium and calm. Pick the one lead feeling — a hint of a second is fine, but one has to drive.

✓ Do this

"Warm and welcoming." — one clear feeling that matches a Friend personality. Every choice now has a test to pass.

✕ Not that

"Premium but playful and minimal but bold." — four feelings fighting. Nothing can satisfy all of them.

The trap: a feeling that contradicts your personality. If they disagree, one is wrong — fix it here, before it infects every color and font.

2

Color — one hero, plus neutrals

Color is the system that does the most work: one hero color, one near-black for text, one light tone for backgrounds, and maybe one accent.

Why it matters. Your hero color is the single fastest way people recognize you. One consistent signature beats a busy palette every time.

How to choose yours

Pick ONE hero — your signature, used for buttons, links, and highlights. Add a near-black for text and a light/paper tone for backgrounds; those two cover most of every page. Give everything a hex code so it's exact. And check contrast: if your hero is pale, white button text won't read on it — darken it for buttons or keep it for highlights only.

✓ Do this

One hero (#db0063), near-black text, light background. Tight and readable.

✕ Not that

Five "brand colors" of equal weight. Now nothing is the signature, and every asset guesses which to use.

The trap: treating five colors as equals because choosing one feels limiting. The limit is the point — it's what makes you recognizable.

3

Typography — two fonts, two jobs

Typography is usually just two fonts: a display font for headings that carries personality, and a body font for paragraphs that's easy to read.

Why it matters. Fonts set tone before a single word is read, and mismatched or too-many fonts are the fastest way to look amateur.

How to choose yours

Pick a characterful display font for headings and a clean, simple body font for reading. Sometimes one font in two weights is enough — never more than two families. Then set a simple size ladder: a big headline, a section heading, and body text. Free sources like Google Fonts cover almost everyone.

✓ Do this

Headings in a bold display font, body in a clean, plain font. Contrast, and both easy to read.

✕ Not that

Three fonts, plus a script for body paragraphs nobody can actually read. Messy and hard work.

The trap: using a beautiful heading font for body text. Character in headings, clarity in body — never swap those jobs.

4

Logo & Wordmark — the mark and its rules

Your logo (or a clean wordmark — your name set in your display font) plus the simple rules for using it.

Why it matters. A consistent mark, used correctly, signals you're established. And you don't need a designed logo to start — a tidy wordmark is a completely legitimate brand mark.

How to choose yours

If you have a logo you like, document its colors and make sure it works on both light and dark backgrounds. If you don't, start with a wordmark. Either way, set three rules: clear space (breathing room around it), minimum size (don't shrink it to a blur), and versions (a light-background one and a dark-background one, so it never disappears).

✓ Do this

A clean wordmark in your display font, with clear-space and light/dark versions defined.

✕ Not that

Waiting months for the "perfect" logo while your brand looks different on every asset in the meantime.

The trap: stalling the whole brand on a logo. Consistency beats artwork — a simple wordmark used everywhere outperforms a great logo used inconsistently.

5

Imagery & Texture — the feel

The style of your photos and graphics, plus any simple recurring element — a shape, a line, a grid.

Why it matters. Two brands can share a hero color and look nothing alike because of imagery. It's a huge part of the feeling, and it's often the most overlooked.

How to choose yours

Match the imagery to your feeling: bright and candid for warm brands, moody and minimal for premium ones, bold and graphic for high-energy ones. Then pick at most one recurring element that shows up across your stuff. Consistency matters more than variety.

✓ Do this

Bright, real, human photos on a warm brand — plus one signature shape used sparingly.

✕ Not that

Generic stock handshakes on a premium brand, with five different graphic styles competing.

The trap: imagery that fights the feeling. Cheap stock on a premium brand quietly undercuts everything else you got right.

6

Components — the repeating pieces

The handful of small pieces you reuse constantly: buttons, headings, callout boxes, and links.

Why it matters. Set these once and everything you make matches automatically. It's the difference between rebuilding the look each time and just assembling it.

How to choose yours

Buttons in your hero color with readable text — one primary style, maybe one outline style. Headings in your display font following the size ladder. Callout boxes as a simple bordered box for tips and key points. Links in your hero color so they're obvious. That's the whole set.

✓ Do this

One button style, one heading style, one callout style — reused on every asset.

✕ Not that

A new button shape and color on every page because nothing was ever set as the standard.

The trap: reinventing components each time. Decide once; reuse forever.

7

Consistency Rules — the guardrails

A short do/don't list that keeps you on brand when you're moving fast.

Why it matters. Rules you can recite are rules you'll actually follow. A five-line list beats a fifty-page brand bible nobody opens.

How to choose yours

Keep it to the few that matter: one hero color, two fonts max, logo gets clear space and never light-on-light, images match the feeling, and when in doubt add white space, not more stuff. Trim it to what you'll remember.

The trap: writing rules so long you never read them again. Short and firm beats thorough and ignored.

A Worked Example

Here's a "warm and welcoming" health coach turning her personality into a system.

Feeling: warm and welcoming — matching her Friend personality.

Color: a warm coral hero (#e8735a), soft charcoal text, a cream background.

Type: a rounded display font for headings, a clean simple font for body — two fonts, both easy to read.

Logo: a wordmark of her name in the coral, with a dark version for light photos.

Imagery: bright, candid photos of real moms and real kitchens — never stock.

Now her website, her PDFs, and her posts all feel like the same warm, welcoming brand — and a new mom recognizes her the second time she sees her.

(Illustrative example — it shows the level of decision, not a palette to copy.)

The Hard Rules

These keep your look consistent and repeatable, and the Builder holds you to them.

One hero color. Neutrals do the heavy lifting; the hero is your signature. Never a pile of equals.

Two fonts, never more. A characterful heading, a readable body. A third font is where brands get messy.

Readable always wins. If people can't read it, it doesn't matter how it looks. Contrast and clarity first.

Every choice ties to personality. No random colors or fonts — each one serves your feeling.

A mark with rules. A logo or a wordmark, with clear space, minimum size, and light/dark versions.

Imagery matches the feeling. No cheap stock on a premium brand, no moody minimalism on a warm one.

Consistency over variety. A few choices repeated everywhere beat a big kit used at random.

When in doubt, white space. More room, not more stuff. Space reads as premium and calm.

Common Mistakes & Fixes

Almost every messy-looking brand falls into one of these.

1. Too many colors

What it looks like: five or six "brand colors," all used equally.

Why it happens: picking one feels limiting, so you keep your options open.

Why it costs you: with no clear signature, nothing is recognizable and every asset guesses.

The fix: choose ONE hero color. Demote the rest to neutrals or a single accent.

2. A font zoo

What it looks like: three, four, or five fonts across your materials.

Why it happens: a new font feels like a fresh, creative touch each time.

Why it costs you: more than two fonts reads as amateur and disjointed.

The fix: lock two — a display for headings, a clean one for body — and stop there.

3. Choosing on looks, not personality

What it looks like: a trendy palette that has nothing to do with who you are.

Why it happens: you saw a brand you liked and copied its style.

Why it costs you: a borrowed look doesn't fit, so your visuals and your message feel off.

The fix: start from your personality and feeling. Your look should be yours, not someone else's.

4. Waiting on the "perfect" logo

What it looks like: the whole brand on hold until a logo is finished.

Why it happens: it feels like you can't have a real brand without a real logo.

Why it costs you: months of inconsistent-looking assets while you wait.

The fix: start with a clean wordmark today. A consistent wordmark beats a great logo used late.

5. Ignoring contrast

What it looks like: pale text on a pale background, or white text on a light hero color.

Why it happens: it looks nice on your screen, so you don't notice the problem.

Why it costs you: people literally can't read it — especially on phones — so your message is lost.

The fix: keep strong contrast — dark text on light, light text only on a dark-enough hero.

6. Mismatched imagery

What it looks like: generic stock photos slapped onto a brand with a clear feeling.

Why it happens: stock is fast and free, so it's the path of least resistance.

Why it costs you: cheap or off-feel imagery undercuts every other good choice you made.

The fix: define your image style up front and only use images that match the feeling.

Is It Ready?

This foundation isn't scored out of 25 — a visual system is judged by whether it's consistent, distinctive, legible, and documented. So it passes a Ready Check: every item a clear yes.

  • You've named one clear feeling, and it matches your personality.
  • You have exactly one hero color, plus a near-black and a light tone — all with hex codes.
  • Your text stays readable — contrast is strong on backgrounds and buttons.
  • You have at most two fonts (or one in two weights), with a simple size ladder.
  • Your body font is genuinely easy to read.
  • You have a documented mark — logo or wordmark — with clear-space and light/dark rules.
  • Your imagery style matches the feeling, with at most one recurring element.
  • Your buttons, headings, callouts, and links are defined once.
  • You have a short do/don't list you could actually follow.
  • The consistency test: a non-designer could keep all of this the same across ten assets.

All boxes checked? Your Visual Style Guide is locked — and every asset you make from here will look like one brand.
